Cyberbullying and online hate — what can you do?

Last reviewed: 12 July 2026 · updated: 13 July 2026

In short: Someone insults, threatens or shames you online. This is not okay, and it is not your fault. First things first: save evidence (screenshots with link and date) before you report or block anything. After that you can report, block, file a police report and get help.

First aid:

  • Save evidence first. Take screenshots before you delete, report or block anything.
  • Do not reply. Do not react to provocations — that often makes things worse.
  • If you are in immediate danger or clearly threatened: police 110.
  • Talk to someone. An attack online is a heavy burden — you do not have to cope with it alone.
01

Save evidence

Save everything before you report or block.

  • Screenshot of every insulting or threatening post.
  • Make sure these are visible: date and time, the username and the context.
  • Write down the internet address (URL) of the post and the profile.
  • The platform (for example Instagram, TikTok) should also be recognisable.
02

Report to the platform and block

  • Report through the platform's reporting function (usually via the three dots on the post).
  • Only block after you have saved the evidence.
  • If the platform does not react, the Bundesnetzagentur has been the central complaints body since the EU Digital Services Act (DSA): dsc.bund.de ↗ (the earlier NetzDG was largely replaced by the DSA in 2024).
03

File a report with the police

  • Insults and threats can be criminal offences.
  • File a report at a police station or online via the Online-Wache of your federal state (onlinewache.polizei.de ↗).
  • Bring the evidence you saved.

Possible offences: § 185 (insult), § 186/187 (defamation/slander), § 241 (threat), § 130 StGB (incitement to hatred). Whether an offence has been committed in a given case is checked by the police and public prosecutor.

04

Protect yourself and get help

  • Set your accounts to private; check who can see your posts.
  • Give yourself a break from the platform — waves of attacks are often strongest at the start.
  • Free advice, for example from HateAid (digital violence).
  • If it weighs on you emotionally: TelefonSeelsorge, around the clock, anonymous.

Common questions

Should I block the person?

Yes, but only after you have saved evidence. Otherwise you may not be able to see the post later.

Why should I not just delete everything?

Because you need the content as evidence — for the report and a possible police report. Save it before you remove it from your view.

Do I have to reply to insults?

No. You do not have to justify yourself. A reply often leads to more attacks. Focus on saving evidence, reporting and getting help.

Is a police report worth it if the person is anonymous?

Yes, it can be worth it. The police and public prosecutor have ways to look behind anonymous profiles. Your screenshots and links help with this.

What if the platform does not delete the post?

Contact the Bundesnetzagentur as the DSA complaints body (dsc.bund.de) if a platform does not remove unlawful content.

Who helps if it is really getting to me?

You do not have to carry this alone. HateAid gives free advice on digital violence; TelefonSeelsorge is there around the clock; Weisser Ring helps victims of crime.

Do you need help?

  • Police emergency: 110 — in immediate danger
  • HateAid — digital violence: 030 252 088 38 (phone hours Mon, Tue, Thu; exact times and appointments at hateaid.org), hateaid.org ↗
  • TelefonSeelsorge: 0800 111 0 111 — free, anonymous, around the clock
  • Hilfetelefon „Gewalt gegen Frauen“ ↗: 116 016 — free, around the clock
  • Weisser Ring ↗ — victim helpline: 116 006 — every day 7 am–10 pm
  • DSA complaints body: dsc.bund.de ↗
