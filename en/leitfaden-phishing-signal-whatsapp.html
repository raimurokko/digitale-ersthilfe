Spot and stop phishing on Signal and WhatsApp

Last reviewed: 12 July 2026 · updated: 13 July 2026

In short: Attackers send a QR code in a message. If you scan it, you secretly link your account to another device. This guide shows in 4 steps how to spot the attack, save your account, keep evidence and file a police report.

Background: This trick is used especially by state attackers from Russia ("Star Blizzard" / "Coldriver") against activists, journalists, diplomats and members of the military. But the method works against anyone who uses Signal or WhatsApp.

Important — please read first:

  • Never scan a QR code from an unexpected message — not even from contacts you know. Call the person first and ask.
  • Do not delete the message right away. Take a screenshot first. The message is important evidence.
  • Your safety comes first. If you feel threatened, contact the police (110) or a support service.
01

Spot the attack

The attackers use a normal feature of the messengers: Signal and WhatsApp let you use one account on another device as well. To do this, you scan a QR code. The attackers send a fake QR code and hope that you scan it.

Common disguises:

  • A fake security check: A message says you must "confirm" or "verify" your account, or it will be blocked.
  • A fake group invitation: A supposed invitation link leads to a website with a QR code. The website looks like Signal or WhatsApp.
  • A message from contacts you know: An account that is already hijacked forwards the QR code to its own contacts. The sender looks familiar.
  • Urgency and pressure: "Today only", "Or you lose your account", "Important — act now". Trustworthy providers never work with time pressure.

Rule of thumb: Signal and WhatsApp never send you a QR code to scan inside a chat. If you want to link another device, you create the QR code yourself in the settings.

02

Take immediate action

Check now whether an unknown device is linked to your account. It only takes a few seconds.

On Signal:

  • Open Signal.
  • Tap your profile picture at the top left.
  • Choose Settings.
  • Tap Linked devices.
  • Check every device in the list. Don't know a device? Tap it and choose Remove.

On WhatsApp:

  • Open WhatsApp.
  • Tap Settings (bottom right).
  • Choose Linked devices.
  • Tap each unknown device and then Log out.

Extra protection:

  • Signal: Settings → Account → turn on Registration Lock. Set a PIN that only you know.
  • WhatsApp: Settings → Account → turn on Two-step verification. Set a six-digit PIN.
  • Update your phone and app: Install all available updates.

Good to know: Once you have disconnected the unknown session, the attacker can no longer read your new messages. But messages they already read may still stay in their copy.

03

Save evidence

Evidence is key for the police report. Record everything you still have.

What you should document:

  • A screenshot of the harmful message with the QR code.
  • A screenshot of the sender's profile (name, phone number, profile picture, timestamp).
  • A screenshot of the linked devices before you remove them: device name, operating system, last activity.
  • A time log: When did the message arrive? When did you (perhaps) scan the code? When did you disconnect the session?

Important: Save all screenshots outside the affected app — for example in a cloud storage with its own password or on a USB stick.

04

Report it

Now it is about making the crime official. This protects other people and helps the authorities spot the pattern.

File a police report:

  • Use the police Online-Wache (online reporting) or go to a police station.
  • Name these offences: data espionage (§ 202a StGB) and computer fraud (§ 263a StGB).
  • Bring all screenshots and the time log.

Inform the BSI:

  • BSI-Bürger-Hotline: 0800 274 1000 (free, Mon–Fri 8 am–6 pm).
  • The BSI collects attack data and can give you technical advice.

Report inside the app:

  • Signal: In the chat, press and hold the message → Report and block.
  • WhatsApp: In the chat, tap the sender → scroll down → Report contact.

Warn your contacts:

  • If your account was hijacked, the attackers may have forwarded the message to your contacts. Actively tell your close contacts — a phone call is best.

Special target groups: If you work as a journalist, an activist, a member of a non-governmental organisation (NGO), in a public authority or in the military, also report the incident to the Central Cybercrime Contact Point (Zentrale Ansprechstelle Cybercrime, ZAC) of your federal state and to your internal IT security team.

Background: How does the trick work technically?

Signal and WhatsApp let one account run on several devices at the same time — for example a smartphone and a laptop. This is what the "Linked devices" feature is for (called "Gekoppelte Geräte" in Signal and "Verknüpfte Geräte" in WhatsApp). If you want to link a new device, the app creates a QR code. The other device scans the code — and is then connected to the account.

The attackers do exactly this: they create a QR code on their device and send it to you — dressed up as a security warning or an invitation. If you scan the code, you link your account to the attackers' device. The attackers then read along with all your new messages.

The Dutch intelligence service AIVD and the German BND have seen this method mostly with Russian state actors. But criminal groups copy it too.
