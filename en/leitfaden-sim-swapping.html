SIM swapping — someone took over my phone number. What to do?

Last reviewed: 13 July 2026 · updated: 13 July 2026

In short: With SIM swapping, offenders move your phone number onto a SIM card of their own — and so they catch your SMS codes (TAN, two-factor). A typical sign is suddenly "no signal". In 4 steps: contact your provider right away, secure your bank and accounts, switch your two-factor sign-in, report it to the police.

First aid:

  • Suddenly "no signal" for no reason? Act at once — this can be SIM swapping.
  • From another phone, call your mobile provider and have the number blocked or brought back to you.
  • Tell your bank at once and have online banking blocked.
01

Contact your mobile provider right away

  • Call from another phone (your own has no signal any more) — the provider's customer hotline, the number is in your contract or on their website.
  • Report the unauthorised SIM change and have the number blocked or brought back to you.
  • Ask about the cause: get them to confirm that no other SIM is active on your number.
02

Secure your bank and payments

  • Tell your bank at once and have online banking blocked (your bank's emergency number).
  • Check your account activity: watch for unknown transfers and dispute them with your bank.
  • Block your cards: through your bank or the central blocking hotline 116 116.
03

Secure your accounts (from a clean device)

  • Email first: change the password, then do the same for your other important accounts. Everywhere, sign out on "all devices".
  • Replace SMS codes: switch your two-factor sign-in from SMS to an authenticator app (e.g. Aegis, 2FAS) or a hardware key.
  • Data-leak check: check at HPI Identity Leak Checker ↗ or Have I Been Pwned ↗ which of your data is out there.
04

Report it and prevent it

  • Report it: to the police or online through the police Online-Wache ↗, with your evidence (the time of the signal loss, your account activity).
  • Possible offences: depending on the case § 263a StGB (computer fraud), § 269 StGB (forgery of data of evidential value). The police decide how to classify it.
  • Prevent it: set a customer or porting password with your provider; for important accounts, stop using SMS codes.

Frequently asked questions

How do I recognise SIM swapping?

Warning signs are a sudden loss of signal (“No service”) for no reason, calls and texts that stop coming in, messages about password changes or unknown login attempts, and a sudden loss of access to your accounts.

Why is SIM swapping so dangerous?

Through your number the offenders catch SMS codes — that means TANs for online banking and two-factor codes. With them they can take over accounts and start transfers.

What comes first?

First get your number back from your mobile provider or have it blocked. After that secure your bank and accounts and switch your two-factor sign-in.

How do I protect myself in future?

Set a customer or porting password with your mobile provider. For important accounts, use an authenticator app or a hardware key instead of SMS codes.

How did the offenders get my data?

Often from phishing or data leaks. Check your email address with the HPI Identity Leak Checker or Have I Been Pwned and change any affected passwords.
