Ransomware — my device is locked or my files are encrypted. What to do?

Last reviewed: 13 July 2026 · updated: 13 July 2026

In short: Ransomware has locked your device or encrypted your files and is demanding money. In 4 steps: disconnect from the network right away, do not pay any ransom, identify the type and check for decryption, restore from a clean backup, and report it to the police.

First aid:

  • Do not pay any ransom.
  • Disconnect the device from the network right away (turn off Wi-Fi, unplug the network cable) and remove connected USB sticks and external hard drives.
  • Do not delete anything and do not rush to reinstall — you may need the data for a later, free decryption.
01

Disconnect right away

  • Disconnect from the internet: Turn off Wi-Fi and unplug the network cable — this stops the ransomware from spreading further.
  • Remove storage media: Disconnect USB sticks, external hard drives and memory cards.
  • Check other devices: Check other devices on the same network and any network drives.
  • Do not rush: Do not delete anything and do not reinstall the device yet.
02

Do not pay any ransom

Do not pay. There is no guarantee that you will get your data back — and every payment funds more crimes. The BSI and Europol clearly advise against it.

A lock screen instead of encryption? If your device only shows a "police" or "BKA" lock message, this is a lock trojan — authorities do not lock devices. Such locks can often be removed in safe mode or with a rescue system. When in doubt, get professional help.

03

Identify the type and check for decryption

  • Identify the ransomware: Use the "Crypto Sheriff" from No More Ransom ↗ (upload one encrypted file and the ransom note) to find out which ransomware it is.
  • Free decryption: No More Ransom offers free decryption tools for many ransomware families. As an alternative, ID Ransomware ↗ helps with identification.
  • Keep the encrypted files: Even if there is no tool today — keep the files; decryptors often appear later.
  • Be careful: Do not use dodgy "decryption services" from the internet — that is often the next scam.
04

Restore, report, secure

  • Restore cleanly: Reinstall the system and load a clean backup (from before the infection). Do not restore backups that may be infected.
  • Change passwords: from a clean device, especially for email and online banking.
  • Report it: to the police or online through the police online report (Online-Wache) ↗. You can also report the incident to the BSI: 0800 274 1000.
  • Possible offences: depending on the case § 253 StGB (extortion), § 303a/§ 303b StGB (data tampering/computer sabotage). The police decide how to classify it.

Frequently asked questions

Should I pay the ransom?

No. There is no guarantee that you will get your data back, and you fund crime. The BSI and Europol clearly advise against it.

My screen is locked with a "police" or "BKA" message — is that real?

No. This is ransomware (a lock screen). Authorities do not lock devices and do not demand money through a screen message. Such locks can often be removed in safe mode or with a rescue system.

Can I save my encrypted files?

Maybe. Use No More Ransom (Crypto Sheriff) to check which ransomware it is — for many families there are free decryption tools. Keep the encrypted files in case a tool appears only later.

How did the ransomware get onto my device?

Often through phishing attachments or links, fake downloads, or unpatched security holes. After cleaning up, updates, regular backups and caution with attachments help.

What about my other devices?

Check all devices on the same network and all connected storage media. Ransomware often spreads to connected devices and network drives.
