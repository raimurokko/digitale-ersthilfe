Cybergrooming and cyberbullying — how do I protect my child, and what can young people do themselves?

Last reviewed: 12 July 2026 · updated: 13 July 2026

In short: Cybergrooming means: an adult contacts a child online in order to sexually harass them. Cyberbullying means: someone is insulted, shamed or shut out online — often by people their own age. In both cases: stay calm, believe the child, save evidence (delete nothing), end the contact and get help. The child is never to blame.

Content note

Content note: This text talks about sexual approaches to children and young people on the internet. In acute danger, call 110 right away.

First aid:

  • In acute danger, call 110 right away.
  • Stay calm and believe the child. No blame. It was right to confide in you.
  • Do NOT delete chats and pictures — take screenshots as evidence. Exception: do not save or forward intimate pictures of a child yourself (talk to the police first).
  • End the contact: block the person and report them to the platform.
01

Talk calmly and listen

Stay calm and listen. Do not blame anyone — not the child and not yourself. Say clearly: "You are not to blame." This keeps the trust, and your child will dare to keep talking to you. This is true for cybergrooming and for bullying.

02

Save evidence (delete nothing)

Delete nothing. Save the chat history, messages and profiles with screenshots (date, time, user name, platform). For intimate pictures of a child: do not download or forward them yourself — talk to the police first.

03

End the contact and report it

  • Block the person and report the profile to the platform.
  • Also report suspicious content to jugendschutz.net (report a violation ↗).
04

File a report and get advice

File a report — with the police or online through the Online-Wache of your federal state. Cybergrooming is a criminal offence (§ 176b StGB ↗). Get advice: Nummer gegen Kummer (children/young people 116 111, parents 0800 111 0 550) or juuuport (young people advise young people). For intimate pictures of under-18s, "Take It Down" can help.

Common questions

How do I recognise cybergrooming?

An adult builds trust through chats, networks or online games: lots of compliments, pushing for secrecy, asking for photos or secret meetings. The child may seem withdrawn.

Should I delete the chat so it stops?

No. The chat is important evidence for the police. Block and report the person — but do not delete the history.

Is cybergrooming a criminal offence?

Yes. § 176b StGB ↗ makes it a crime to influence a child in order to lead them to sexual acts. The exact classification must be checked legally.

My child is being bullied online by classmates — what helps?

Document everything with screenshots, involve the school and a trusted person, report the content to the platform. Advice at juuuport and Nummer gegen Kummer.

I am a young person and I am being torn apart online. What can I do?

You are not to blame. Save evidence (screenshots), do not answer provocations, block and report the person. Talk to a trusted adult or anonymously at juuuport or on 116 111.

Someone shared an intimate picture of me (under 18) — what now?

This is not your fault. The free, anonymous service "Take It Down" can help stop the spread, without you having to send the picture. Get help from a trusted person and consider filing a report.

Do you need help?

  • Police emergency: 110 — in acute danger; you can also file a report through the Online-Wache of your federal state
  • Nummer gegen Kummer ↗ — helpline for children and young people: 116 111 — free, anonymous
  • Nummer gegen Kummer — parents' helpline: 0800 111 0 550 — free, anonymous
  • juuuport: advice from young people for young people — juuuport.de ↗
  • jugendschutz.net: report violations — jugendschutz.net ↗
  • Take It Down (pictures of under-18s): takeitdown.ncmec.org ↗
