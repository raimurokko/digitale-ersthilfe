Digital separation: secure your accounts, devices and location after a breakup

Last reviewed: 12 July 2026 · updated: 13 July 2026

In short: After a breakup, the other person may have had access to your accounts, your phone or your location. You can end this access step by step: set new passwords, turn on two-factor protection, stop location and family sharing. The right order matters, because the other person may notice some steps.

First aid:

  • Use a safe device that the other person cannot access (for example at a friend's place, at a support service or at the library).
  • Protect your email inbox first. Your main email is the key to almost all other accounts.
  • Make a plan, do not rush. Some steps can be noticed — if there is danger, get advice first.
  • In acute danger: police 110. For advice: Hilfetelefon 116 016.
01

Take stock and choose a safe device

  • Write down: Which devices do you use (phone, laptop, tablet, smart home, Wi-Fi router)? Which important accounts (email, cloud, social networks, online banking, streaming)?
  • Mark where the other person might know the password or have access.
  • Choose a safe device that the other person does not control.
  • If you suspect secret surveillance, read the stalkerware guide first — removing it can raise an alarm.
02

Secure your email and important accounts

  • New, strong passwords — a separate one for each account. Start with your main email.
  • Turn on two-factor protection (2FA) — with an app or number that only you can access. The BSI clearly recommends this.
  • Check your recovery email and phone number and change them to your own. With Google, codes can still go to the old details for up to 7 days after a change.
  • Check your active sessions and devices and sign out anything you do not know.
03

Stop location and family sharing

  • Google location sharing: Google Maps → profile picture → "Location sharing" → person → "Stop".
  • Apple "Find My": Settings → your name → "Find My" / Family Sharing → turn off "Share My Location".
  • Leave Apple Family Sharing: Settings → Family → your name → "Stop Using". As the organiser, you may need to break up the group.
  • Android "Find My Device": shared device → Settings → "Stop sharing".

Remember: When you stop a sharing setting, the other person may notice. Pick the moment carefully.

04

Split what is shared and check social media

  • Shared accounts: cloud/photos, password manager, subscriptions (streaming), smart home. Sign out, change the password or set up your own new accounts.
  • Shared mobile phone contract: Find out who holds the contract — that person can often see bills and location services. If needed, get your own contract or SIM.
  • Social media: check linked apps and permissions and remove anything you do not know; set your privacy tighter.
  • Router / Wi-Fi: change the Wi-Fi password so no one can listen in through the home network.

Common questions

Can the other person notice that I changed the password?

Yes, that is possible. If the person is still signed in, they may be signed out or get a security alert. If there is danger, think beforehand whether and when you do this.

In what order should I do this?

First take stock and get a safe device, then your email and important accounts, then location and family sharing, and last shared accounts and social media. If you suspect stalkerware, read up on that first.

Do I have to change my password regularly?

No. The BSI says: a strong, unique password plus two-factor protection is better than changing it often. You should change it when someone might know your password — often the case after a breakup.

What is two-factor authentication (2FA)?

A second layer of protection on top of the password, for example a code from an app or a fingerprint. Even if someone knows your password, they cannot get into the account without the second factor.

What do I do about a shared mobile phone contract?

Find out who holds the contract — that person can often see connection data and location services. If you can, switch to your own contract or your own SIM card.

I am afraid the person may become violent. What comes first?

Your safety comes first. First talk to a support service about a safety plan (for example Hilfetelefon 116 016). In acute danger, call the police on 110.

Do you need help?

  • Police emergency: 110 — in acute danger, around the clock
  • Hilfetelefon „Gewalt gegen Frauen“ ↗: 116 016 — free, anonymous, around the clock, many languages
  • Weisser Ring ↗ — victim support line: 116 006 — free, every day 7 am–10 pm
  • TelefonSeelsorge: 0800 111 0 111 — free, around the clock
  • bff — active against digital violence: support services and information at frauen-gegen-gewalt.de ↗
