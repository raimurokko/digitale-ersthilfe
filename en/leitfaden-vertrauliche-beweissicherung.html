Confidential evidence collection after violence

Last reviewed: 12 July 2026 · updated: 13 July 2026

In short: After an act of violence, you can have a medical examination and secure evidence — even without involving the police. The evidence is stored safely. You decide later, calmly, whether you want to report the crime.

Legal basis: Since 1 March 2020, health insurers in many federal states pay for these services under § 27 Abs. 1a SGB V.

Please read first:

  • If you can, do not wash yourself and do not change your clothes before evidence is secured. Keep the clothes you wore in a paper bag (not a plastic bag).
  • If you are injured or in immediate danger: Call 112 (medical emergency) or 110 (police).
  • You are not alone. The Hilfetelefon Sexueller Missbrauch (sexual abuse helpline, 0800 22 55 530) and the Hilfetelefon Gewalt gegen Frauen (violence against women helpline, 08000 116 016) are here for you.
01

What is confidential evidence collection?

After an act of violence, evidence is often only visible or provable for a short time. Confidential evidence collection helps you secure this evidence — without having to decide right away whether you want to report the crime.

What does „confidential“ mean?

  • The clinic or outpatient unit does not inform the police.
  • The evidence (for example photos, swabs, written notes) is stored safely.
  • You decide whether and when you report the crime. In most federal states, the evidence is kept for up to 10 years.

Why so fast? Some traces can no longer be detected after a few hours. Try to have the examination within 72 hours of the act. Even later, photos of bruises or written notes are still valuable.

02

Where can I get help?

In Germany there are Gewaltschutzambulanzen (violence protection outpatient clinics) and partner hospitals that offer the examination. Not every hospital offers this — it helps to look for the right place on purpose.

How to find a place near you:

  • ODABS — database for confidential evidence collection (search by postcode, 7 languages) ↗
  • Soforthilfe nach Vergewaltigung (immediate help after rape) — regions Baden-Württemberg, Hesse, Rhineland-Palatinate, Saxony ↗
  • Hilfetelefon Gewalt gegen Frauen ↗: 08000 116 016 — around the clock, connects you to places near you.
  • Hilfetelefon Sexueller Missbrauch: 0800 22 55 530

Tip: You may bring a person you trust. The examination is free for you.

03

How does the examination work?

You decide what is examined and documented. You can take breaks at any time or stop the examination.

What usually happens:

  • Talk: The doctor asks you calmly what happened. Only what you want to tell is written down.
  • Physical examination: Injuries are photographed and written down.
  • Securing evidence: If needed, swabs are taken and your clothes are kept for storage.
  • Medical care: Wounds are treated. If needed, you get advice on medicine against HIV infection („PEP“), the „morning-after pill“, and sexually transmitted infections.

After the examination:

  • You get a report for yourself and information about other counselling services.
  • The evidence is stored in encrypted form. You get an ID so you can access it later.

You are in control: You do not have to do anything you do not want. You may also have only part of the examination done.

04

If you decide to report the crime later

You can report the crime days, weeks or years later. As long as the time limit is running, your evidence is kept safe.

Time limits (statute of limitations):

  • Rape (§ 177 StGB ↗): usually 20 years, and often much longer for minors.
  • Bodily harm (§ 223 StGB ↗): 5 years.
  • Serious bodily harm (§ 226 StGB ↗): 20 years.

How to report the crime:

  • Online-Wache der Polizei (police online station) ↗ — report a crime online.
  • Or go to a police station. You may take a person you trust with you.
  • The police can request the evidence from the clinic — but only if you want that.

Support in the meantime:

  • Weißer Ring: 116 006 — victim support, including referral to lawyers.
  • Many women's counselling centres offer free support through the criminal proceedings (called psychosoziale Prozessbegleitung).

Good to know: Reporting the crime is your right, not a duty. Even without a report, you can get counselling and medical care.
